href="/wiki/physics" title="physics">physics</a>, <b>spacetime</b> is any <a href="/wiki/mathematical_model" title="mathematical model">mathematical model</a> which fuses the <a href="/wiki/three-dimensional_space" title="three-dimensional space">three dimensions of space</a> and the one <a href="/wiki/dimension" title="dimension">dimension</a> of <a href="/wiki/time" title="time">time</a> into a single <a href="/wiki/four-dimensional" class="mw-redirect" title="four-dimensional">four-dimensional</a> <a href="/wiki/manifold" title="manifold">manifold</a>. <a href="/wiki/minkowski_diagram" class="mw-redirect" title="minkowski diagram">spacetime diagrams</a> can be used to visualize <a href="/wiki/special_relativity" title="special relativity">relativistic</a> effects, such as why different observers perceive differently where and when events occur.\n</p><p>until the 20th century, it was assumed that the three-dimensional geometry of the universe (its spatial expression in terms of coordinates, distances, and directions) was independent of one-dimensional time. the physicist <a href="/wiki/albert_einstein" title="albert einstein">albert einstein</a> helped develop the idea of spacetime as part of his <a href="/wiki/theory_of_relativity" title="theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>. prior to his pioneering work, scientists had two separate theories to explain physical phenomena: <a href="/wiki/isaac_newton" title="isaac newton">isaac newton</a>\'s laws of physics described the motion of massive objects, while <a href="/wiki/james_clerk_maxwell" title="james clerk maxwell">james clerk maxwell</a>\'s electromagnetic models explained the properties of light. however, in 1905, einstein based <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/translation:on_the_electrodynamics_of_moving_bodies" class="extiw" title="wikisource:translation:on the electrodynamics of moving bodies">a work on special relativity</a> on two postulates:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the laws of physics are <a href="/wiki/invariant_(physics)" title="invariant (physics)">invariant</a> (i.e., identical) in all <a href="/wiki/inertial_frame_of_reference" title="inertial frame of reference">inertial systems</a> (i.e., non-accelerating frames of reference)</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/speed_of_light" title="speed of light">speed of light</a> in a <a href="/wiki/vacuum" title="vacuum">vacuum</a> is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the light source.</li></ul>\n<p>the logical consequence of taking these postulates together is the inseparable joining of the four dimensions\xe2\x80\x94hitherto assumed as independent\xe2\x80\x94of space and time. many counterintuitive consequences emerge: in addition to being independent of the motion of the light source, the speed of light is constant regardless of the frame of reference in which it is measured; the distances and even temporal ordering of pairs of events change when measured in different <a href="/wiki/inertial_frame_of_reference" title="inertial frame of reference">inertial frames of reference</a> (this is the <a href="/wiki/relativity_of_simultaneity" title="relativity of simultaneity">relativity of simultaneity</a>); and the linear additivity of velocities no longer holds true.\n</p><p>einstein framed his theory in terms of <a href="/wiki/kinematics" title="kinematics">kinematics</a> (the study of moving bodies). his theory was an advance over <a href="/wiki/history_of_special_relativity#lorentz&#39;s_1904_model" title="history of special relativity">lorentz\'s 1904 theory of electromagnetic phenomena</a> and <a href="/wiki/history_of_special_relativity#poincar\xc3\xa9&#39;s_dynamics_of_the_electron" title="history of special relativity">poincar\xc3\xa9\'s electrodynamic theory</a>. although these theories included equations identical to those that einstein introduced (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/lorentz_transformation" title="lorentz transformation">lorentz transformation</a>), they were essentially ad&#160;hoc models proposed to explain the results of various experiments\xe2\x80\x94including the famous <a href="/wiki/michelson%e2%80%93morley_experiment" title="michelson\xe2\x80\x93morley experiment">michelson\xe2\x80\x93morley interferometer experiment</a>\xe2\x80\x94that were extremely difficult to fit into existing paradigms.\n</p><p>in 1908, <a href="/wiki/hermann_minkowski" title="hermann minkowski">hermann minkowski</a>\xe2\x80\x94once one of the math professors of a young einstein in z\xc3\xbcrich\xe2\x80\x94presented a geometric interpretation of special relativity that fused time and the three spatial dimensions of space into a single four-dimensional continuum now known as <a href="/wiki/minkowski_space" title="minkowski space">minkowski space</a>. a key feature of this interpretation is the formal definition of the spacetime interval. although measurements of distance and time between <a href="/wiki/event_(relativity)" title="event (relativity)">events</a> differ for measurements made in different reference frames, the spacetime interval is independent of the inertial frame of reference in which they are recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>minkowski\'s geometric interpretation of relativity was to prove vital to einstein\'s development of his 1915 <a href="/wiki/general_relativity" title="general relativity">general theory of relativity</a>, wherein he showed how <a href="/wiki/stress%e2%80%93energy_tensor" title="stress\xe2\x80\x93energy tensor">mass and energy</a> <a href="/wiki/riemann_curvature_tensor" title="riemann curvature tensor">curve</a> flat spacetime into a <a href="/wiki/pseudo-riemannian_manifold" title="pseudo-riemannian manifold">pseudo-riemannian manifold</a>.\n</p><p><span class="anchor" id="contents">: 
\n
